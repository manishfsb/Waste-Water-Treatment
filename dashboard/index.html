EDA Overview — 2020–2025

Data Quality

Missing Values by Attribute (2020–2025)

Composite columns have the highest structural missingness. Composite sampling was introduced in 2022, so all composite fields are absent for the entire 2020 and 2021 datasets (full years). Even within 2022–2025, composite tests are not conducted every day, keeping missing rates at 30–50% for those columns. Pre-processing: Do not impute composite fields for 2020–2021 — treat them as a separate analytical layer. For 2022–2025, impute cautiously or use indicator features.

Effluent Distributions — Grab Samples (BOD, COD, TSS, pH)

BOD and TSS are tightly clustered well below their 10 mg/L limits on most days, confirming good treatment performance. COD shows a wider spread with a heavier right tail. Year-on-year shifts in violin width or median position indicate changes in treatment quality or load. 2020 data covers only part of the year (92 days).

Effluent Distributions — Composite Samples (BOD, COD, TSS, pH)

Composite sampling started in 2022, so 2020 and 2021 are absent from these panels — this is expected, not missing data. Composite samples are 24-hour pooled averages, which tend to show narrower distributions than grab samples because short-duration spikes are averaged out over the day. Where composite and grab medians diverge, it suggests intra-day variability in treatment performance. This mismatch is one reason composite effluent targets are harder to predict from point-in-time secondary clarifier readings.

Long-term Operational Trends

Inlet Flow — Monthly Average (MLD)

Strong monsoon signal (June–September): Monthly average flow rises 40–60% above the dry-season baseline each year, driven by stormwater infiltration into the sewage network during Nepal's monsoon. The design capacity of 32.4 MLD provides a useful upper bound for outlier detection. Pre-processing: Add a binary monsoon flag (1 = June–September) or include calendar month as a feature — both will capture a large share of flow variance.

Power Efficiency — Monthly Average (KW/ML)

Inverse relationship with flow: Power/flow (KW/ML) spikes in dry months because fixed energy loads are divided by smaller treated volumes. Values frequently exceed the 482 KW/ML design baseline in January–May. Note: 2021 data covers only NEA grid power (no gas engine record) — consider flagging this as a distinct measurement regime if power is a model feature.

Water Quality Trends — Monthly Averages

Inlet Quality (BOD, COD, TSS)

Concentration-dilution trade-off: Inlet BOD and COD peak during dry months (January–May) when wastewater is less diluted, then drop during the monsoon as flow increases. TSS is more erratic and sensitive to storm events. Outlier note: Very high BOD (>600 mg/L) or COD (>2000 mg/L) days are likely genuine extreme events, not errors — retain but flag them.

Effluent Quality (BOD, COD, TSS)

Generally good treatment: Effluent BOD and TSS stay within the 10 mg/L limit on most days. Effluent COD shows greater variability with periodic exceedances of the 250 mg/L limit, particularly at high load. Note that effluent pH (not shown here — different scale) is typically well within 6.5–8.0. Missing effluent data is more common in earlier years due to incomplete records.

Treatment Performance

Removal Efficiency — Monthly Average (%)

BOD and TSS removal consistently exceed 95%, meeting design targets across all years. COD removal averages 90–97% and is more variable month-to-month, especially during high-load periods. Data quality flag: Days showing removal below 0% (effluent > inlet) or above 100% are measurement or recording errors — exclude or winsorise them before training any predictive model.

Effluent Compliance Rate by Year (%)

COD has the lowest and most variable compliance rate across all years. pH and TSS compliance are generally high and stable. BOD compliance is strong but dips in high-load months. Interpretation caveat: Lower compliance figures in 2021 may partly reflect missing measurement data rather than genuine exceedances — always examine the data-availability rate alongside pass/fail counts.

Seasonal & Annual Patterns

Seasonal Pattern — Monthly Averages (All Years)

Calendar month explains a large share of variance in flow, power efficiency, and inlet concentrations. The wet season (June–September) drives higher flow and lower concentrations; the dry season (October–May) shows the opposite. Year-over-year patterns are broadly consistent, making seasonal decomposition a viable pre-processing step. Recommendation: Include month (1–12) or a wet/dry indicator as a feature in any predictive model.

Annual Averages — Key Parameters (% of Control Limit)

Values normalised to their respective control limits (100% = at limit). This chart lets you compare parameters on a single scale. Inlet parameters hovering near 100% indicate the raw sewage is close to the design load; effluent parameters well below 100% confirm treatment is effective. Watch for year-on-year drift in any parameter trending upward — this may signal catchment changes or equipment aging.
