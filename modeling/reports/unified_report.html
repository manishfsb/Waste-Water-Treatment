▶ Methodology — Cyclical Encoding of Calendar Features

The Discontinuity Problem with Raw Integer Encoding

month (1–12) and day_of_week (0–6) are cyclic: December is adjacent to January, and Sunday wraps back to Monday. As raw integers, a linear model treats the gap between month 12 and month 1 as 11 units — the largest possible distance — creating a spurious discontinuity at the year boundary. The fitted coefficient forces a monotone trend across the full 1–12 range and cannot capture the seasonal "U-shape" or mid-year peaks that several effluent targets exhibit. Tree models (RF, GB, XGB) are unaffected because their splits are ordinal and learned independently per node; the discontinuity never enters their loss.

Encoding Formula

Two orthogonal projections onto the unit circle replace each integer feature. Together, (sin, cos) uniquely identify any cycle position while preserving wrap-around topology: December and January are geometrically close, not 11 units apart.

Original featurePeriod sin columncos column
month (1–12)12 month_sin = sin(2π × month / 12) month_cos = cos(2π × month / 12)
day_of_week (0–6)7 dow_sin = sin(2π × dow / 7) dow_cos = cos(2π × dow / 7)

The raw month and day_of_week columns are dropped before training; the 4 cyclic columns take their place, adding zero rows of missingness. The year column is kept as-is (not cyclic — it encodes genuine secular trend).

Scope and Interpretation Caveat

Cyclic encoding is applied only in Exp3-S2 linear models (OLS, Ridge, ElasticNet). All prior experiments (Exp1, Exp2, Exp3 Sub-1) and all tree-based and advanced models (RF, GB, XGB, Voting, Stacking, Phase 9–11) use raw integer encoding.

Exp3-S2 also introduces new CONSIDER-tier features absent from Exp3-S1, so the two experiments are not a controlled A/B test for encoding alone. Any R² difference in the table below reflects the combined effect of the broader feature set and the encoding change.

▶ VIF Collinearity Analysis — Exp3-S2 Feature Sets

Variance Inflation Factor computed on training rows (year < 2025) after listwise deletion. VIF > 10 = problematic collinearity for linear models. VIF 5–10 = moderate. The threshold rule of thumb: VIF > 10 indicates that the variance of a coefficient estimate is inflated by a factor of 10 relative to an orthogonal design — OLS/Ridge predictions are less reliable for such features.

Global across all 8 datasets: 25 HIGH (VIF>10) · 57 MODERATE (VIF 5–10). Concentrated in Sec Sed vs Sec Clarifier pairs (adjacent measurements of the same parameter) and Aeration SVI (mathematically derived from SV30/MLSS). ElasticNet handles collinearity via L1 penalty — this explains why ElNet outperforms OLS/Ridge on composite targets. OLS/Ridge coefficients are directly inflated by collinear pairs.

Notes on VIF removal
  • Hub pattern: a feature appearing in many "Collinear With" rows (e.g. SVI) is a hub — dropping it alone resolves multiple HIGH VIFs simultaneously. Multiple HIGH flags ≠ remove all; count the distinct hubs first.
  • Tree models (RF, XGB) are unaffected — collinearity only inflates OLS/Ridge coefficient variance. ElasticNet handles it automatically via L1 shrinkage. VIF-based removal is relevant only before fitting OLS or Ridge.

▶ Root-Cause Analysis

1. Sample-to-feature ratio. Worst case: Comp TSS — 290 training rows, 27 features → n/p = 10.7. MLPs require far more data than tree ensembles to generalise from small tabular datasets. Typical guidance: n/p ≥ 100–200 for stable MLP generalisation.

2. Temporal distribution shift. 2025 test data shows different distributional properties than 2021–2024 training data (Q4-Flow and DJF errors 2–3× higher — see Error Regime Decomposition). MLPs form highly non-linear decision boundaries that diverge further from shifted data than regularised linear models or bagged trees.

3. Early stopping did not prevent 2025 failure. Early stopping (validation_fraction=0.10, n_iter_no_change=20) monitors in-sample generalisation on the 2021–2024 fold, not 2025 generalisation. Gaps range from +0.41 (Grab COD) to +6.77 (Comp pH) despite early stopping — confirming that early stopping alone cannot address distributional shift.

4. Network capacity is not the bottleneck. Best architectures are small — (64,) or (128,) single hidden layer with α=1.0 for 6 of 8 targets. The grid search found that more capacity hurts; adding layers would worsen the gaps. The problem is generalisation, not expressiveness.

Future conditions for ANN. Dataset growth to n ≥ 3,000 per target and an LSTM architecture (explicit temporal modelling via hidden state rather than hand-crafted lags) are the recommended prerequisites before revisiting neural networks on this problem.

Overfit-Aware Model Selection

Three complementary rules re-rank every (target × experiment) row beyond naive max(Test R²): gap-adjusted score penalises overfit beyond a 10-pt tolerance; the one-SE rule picks the smallest-gap model within 0.03 R² of the top; the Pareto frontier lists all models not dominated on (R²_test ↑, |gap| ↓). RMSE and MAE are shown alongside R² for operational interpretability — they rank identically to R² within a target but carry real units (mg/L or pH) that relate to discharge consent limits.

Why re-rank?

On this dataset (n_test ≈ 160–280 for the held-out 2025 year), Test R² alone is noisy. A model with R²=0.43 and gap=0.88 is not equivalent to one with R²=0.40 and gap=0.02 — the former is memorising.

Rules applied

Gap-adjusted: score = R²_test − 0.5 · max(0, |gap| − 0.1). Penalises overfit beyond a 10-pt tolerance.

One-SE rule: among models within 0.05 R² of the top, pick the smallest |gap|.

Pareto frontier: models not dominated on (R²_test ↑, |gap| ↓).

RMSE and MAE are shown alongside R² for each rule's winner — they carry the same ranking as R² within a target but are operationally interpretable (mg/L for BOD/COD/TSS, pH units for pH).

Root Cause Analysis

1. Genuine 2025 process change — not a modelling artefact. MAE doubled (8.7 → 18.1 mg/L) while σ-ratio = 0.78, confirming the 2025 test set has comparable variance to training. The model is not hitting a low-variance wall — it is predicting the wrong values. This is the signature of a non-stationarity in the plant's COD removal mechanism that was absent during the 2021–2024 training window.

2. Inlet COD is a weak proxy for effluent COD under the current conditions. Composite effluent COD depends on secondary treatment efficiency, which is governed by MLSS, SVI, and aeration conditions — features with 35–50% missingness on composite rows (CONSIDER tier). When these are absent, the model falls back to inlet + flow, whose MI with effluent Comp COD is low (<0.15 on training data).

3. Small composite sample size amplifies memorisation. Composite targets have only ~515–633 training rows after dropna. With 17–32 features the effective rows-per-feature ratio is 16–37 — below the commonly cited heuristic of 50. Even heavily regularised models (ElNet α=10) risk encoding 2022-era COD spikes that do not recur in 2025.

4. Feature engineering and temporal lags do not help. Phase 10 full FE: best R² = −0.008 (Ridge). Phase 10b selective FE: best = −0.051. Phase 11 temporal lags: best = +0.107 (ElNet, Phase 11) but 2025 MAE remains ~17 mg/L. Every additional feature increases the risk of learning training-specific patterns without improving generalisation.

5. Recommended next steps. (a) Flag Comp COD predictions as unreliable in any operational dashboard until 2025 data is incorporated into training. (b) Collect causal features with higher priority: secondary sludge age (SRT), MLSS, and effluent turbidity have high missingness now but directly govern COD removal — their availability on composite measurement days would be the single highest leverage improvement. (c) Retrain once ≥ 90 new 2025 rows are available (roughly 3 months of composite measurements) and test whether the new regime is stable enough for a combined 2021–2025 model. (d) As an interim measure, a shallow decision tree (max depth 3) trained only on 2024–2025 data is likely to outperform any model trained on the full 2021–2024 window for near-term operational use.
